The Mongol Empire began to fracture after Kublai Khan's death in 1294, collapsing due to succession disputes, the Black Death, and the 1368 overthrow of the Yuan Dynasty by China's Ming Dynasty. The empire split into four, with the Ilkhanate (1335) and Golden Horde (15th century) falling as their power diminished.
Key Reasons for the Fall
Fragmentation: Upon Kublai Khan's death in 1294, the empire split into four independent, often conflicting, khanates (Yuan Dynasty, Golden Horde, Ilkhanate, and Chagatai Khanate).
Succession Disputes: Frequent, violent power struggles weakened the central government.
The Black Death: The plague weakened the population and disrupted trade routes (the Silk Road) that held the empire together.
Loss of Military Edge: Mongol armies became less effective over time, and the Mongols failed to adapt to new tactics used by their subjects.
Assimilation and Rebellion: As Mongols adopted the local cultures of the lands they conquered (e.g., in China and Persia), they lost their nomadic identity. This, combined with local rebellions—such as the uprising that established the Ming Dynasty in 1368—led to their collapse.

The Four Khanates' Decline
Yuan Dynasty (China): Overthrown in 1368 by the Ming Dynasty.
Ilkhanate (Persia): Disintegrated between 1335 and 1353.
Golden Horde (Russia): Lost control in 1480 after the Great Stand on the Ugra River.
Chagatai Khanate (Central Asia): Fragmented in the 17th century.
